# AI Trend Radar Lab

A foresight and learning repository for tracking AI, cybersecurity, technical literacy and weak signals before they become mainstream management topics.

This repository documents a structured way to observe emerging AI-related signals, evaluate their relevance, understand their technical meaning and translate them into organizational implications, governance questions, adoption follow-ups and communication opportunities.

## Core idea

Technological change does not become relevant only when it reaches the mainstream.

By then, organizations are often already reacting.

This lab exists to track early signals before they become obvious, to separate meaningful patterns from noise and to understand what emerging technologies could mean for workflows, decision rights, governance, communication, leadership and adoption.

The goal is not to collect trends for their own sake.

The goal is to build a repeatable radar system for moving from signal to strategic relevance.

## Why this exists

I work at the intersection of AI adoption, UX, internal communication, governance and organizational transformation.

To make AI systems operational, I need more than abstract trend awareness.

I need technical literacy, risk literacy and the ability to understand what emerging technologies could mean for real workflows, responsibilities, trust, security and organizational behavior.

This repository documents that learning process in public.

It is designed to support both human use and future AI-assisted workflows: source mapping, signal evaluation, trend notes, technical concept breakdowns, implication mapping and handoff questions for governance, adoption or content work.

## Trend maturity model

This repository focuses on the early and middle phases of trend development.

```txt
0. Noise
1. Weak signal
2. Emerging pattern
3. Expert debate
4. Early adopter use case
5. Vendor push
6. Management hype
7. Operational pressure
8. Governance requirement
9. Mainstream expectation
```

The radar is especially interested in the moments before a topic becomes mainstream:

* when a weak signal starts repeating
* when experts begin to debate risks and implications
* when vendors start turning a topic into a sales narrative
* when leaders start demanding action before feasibility is clear
* when public debate starts creating pressure inside organizations

## Working questions

This repository is guided by questions such as:

* What is changing technically?
* Is this a real signal, an emerging pattern or just noise?
* Where did the signal appear?
* Which source types are repeating it?
* Why does it matter now?
* What could this mean for organizations?
* Which risks are underestimated?
* Which workflows could be affected?
* What should leaders understand before the topic becomes mainstream?
* What should be watched, tested, governed or communicated?
* Which follow-up belongs in another repository?

## Key modules

### `00-radar-system/`

The method layer of the repository.

This section defines how the radar works: terminology, weak-signal logic, trend maturity levels, source evaluation, signal evaluation and the connection to hype-cycle thinking.

### `01-linkedin-trend-radar/`

A standalone module for using LinkedIn as an early-signal radar.

LinkedIn is treated not only as a publishing platform, but also as a noisy professional signal environment where experts, vendors, researchers, practitioners, leaders and critics reveal early patterns before they reach mainstream management discourse.

This section focuses on feed design, source mapping, noise reduction, signal detection and LinkedIn-specific trend notes.

### `02-trend-topics/`

Worked trend topics and example dossiers.

This section contains exemplary trend topics such as cybersecurity basics for AI adoption, agent risks before mainstream adoption, AI sovereignty, local AI, RAG and AI governance shifts.

Each topic can include early signals, maturity assessment, organizational implications, source notes and action handoffs.

### `03-source-systems/`

The source intelligence layer.

This section documents where signals may come from: LinkedIn, newsletters, research labs, public institutions, media, analysts, vendor blogs, communities and events.

### `04-technical-literacy/`

The technical learning layer.

This section contains glossary entries, concept breakdowns and explainers for non-engineering leadership literacy.

The goal is not to pretend to be a software engineer.

The goal is to understand enough technical context to ask better questions, evaluate risks and make stronger organizational decisions.

### `05-organizational-implications/`

The translation layer.

This section turns technical and trend observations into questions for workflows, leadership, governance, communication and adoption.

### `06-action-and-handoff/`

The follow-up layer.

This section classifies what should happen after a trend has been observed and assessed.

Possible handoffs include:

* watchlist
* technical literacy note
* governance question
* adoption question
* vendor evaluation
* leadership briefing
* content idea
* LinkedIn post
* experiment
* handoff to another repository

### `07-linkedin-bridge/`

The public narrative layer.

This section connects radar notes to LinkedIn posts, carousels, essays and published content.

LinkedIn carries the narrative. GitHub keeps the underlying source map, learning log, concept breakdown, implication map or action note.

## Working principle

This is not a repository for pretending to be a software engineer.

It is a repository for building technical and strategic literacy in public.

The goal is to understand emerging technologies early enough to translate them into better decisions, safer workflows, more credible governance, stronger communication and more realistic AI adoption.

## Role in the portfolio

This is the foresight and learning repository.

It detects early signals, evaluates their maturity and translates them into strategic relevance.

This repository is part of a four-repo portfolio system:

* `ai-trend-radar-lab` — foresight, weak signals and technical learning
* `ai-governance-risk-toolkit` — trust, review, accountability and risk
* `adoption-operating-system` — strategy, operating models and adoption logic
* `ai-content-lab` — applied AI workflows for content and knowledge systems

The four repositories follow a shared cycle:

```txt
Detect
→ Assess
→ Govern
→ Operationalize
→ Communicate
→ Evaluate
→ Recalibrate
```

This repository primarily owns the first part of the cycle:

```txt
Detect
→ Assess early signals
→ Map implications
→ Define handoff questions
```
